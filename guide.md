## 1. Statutory Framework

### 1.1 The Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FD&C Act)

The **Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act** (21 U.S.C. § 301 et seq.) is the foundational statutory authority governing medical device advertising and promotion. Critically, unlike prescription drugs — which have a dedicated advertising regulation at 21 CFR Part 202 — medical device advertising is governed primarily through the **misbranding provisions** of the FD&C Act itself, principally § 502(a) (false or misleading labeling), and, for restricted devices, § 502(q) and (r) (advertising-specific provisions).

🔗 [Full text of the FD&C Act — FDA.gov](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/laws-enforced-by-fda/federal-food-drug-and-cosmetic-act-fdc-act)

### 1.3 Section 502 — Misbranding (The Core Provision)

A device is **misbranded** if it falls under any of the following conditions:

- **§ 502(a):** Labeling is **false or misleading in any particular** — and, as amended by Cures § 3037 and CAA 2023 § 3630, sets the **HCEI** conditions and the **§ 502(gg) PIE** safe harbor (see § 6.4–6.5)
- **§ 502(b):** Label lacks required information (name, quantity, manufacturer/packer/distributor)
- **§ 502(c):** Required label information is not prominently displayed
- **§ 502(f):** Labeling fails to bear **adequate directions for use** or adequate warnings
- **§ 502(n):** (Prescription drugs only — instructive analog) advertising "true statement" requirement; **there is no device equivalent**
- **§ 502(q):** For **restricted devices**: (1) the **advertising is false or misleading in any particular**; or (2) the device is **sold, distributed, or used in violation of regulations** prescribed under § 520(e)
- **§ 502(r):** For **restricted devices**: misbranded unless all advertisements and descriptive printed matter contain **(1) a true statement of the device's established name**, printed prominently and in type at least half as large as any trade or brand name, and **(2) a brief statement of the intended uses of the device and relevant warnings, precautions, side effects, and contraindications** (plus component/formula descriptions for devices the Secretary designates). Enacted by the 1976 Medical Device Amendments; still current law.
- **§§ 502(u)–(v):** Misbranding provisions for reprocessed single-use devices (added by MDUFMA 2002)

> ⚠️ **Key Point:** Misbranding via promotional activity (labeling that is false or misleading, or promotion for off-label uses) can trigger enforcement actions including warning letters, injunctions, seizure, civil money penalties, and criminal prosecution.

## 2. Labeling vs. Advertising

### 2.1 Definition of "Labeling"

Under **FD&C Act § 201(m)** (21 U.S.C. § 321(m)):

> *"The term 'labeling' means all labels and other written, printed, or graphic matter (1) upon any article or any of its containers or wrappers, or (2) accompanying such article."*

The term "accompanying" has been broadly interpreted by courts. **Physical attachment is not required.**

**Landmark cases:**
- ***Kordel v. United States***, 335 U.S. 345 (1948) — labeling includes written materials that supplement or explain a product even when shipped separately, where there is a common origin and integrated distribution.
- ***United States v. Urbuteit***, 335 U.S. 355 (1948) — companion case confirming the broad reach of "labeling" (decided the same day as *Kordel*).

**Materials typically considered labeling include:**
- Package inserts and Instructions for Use (IFU)
- Sales brochures, detail aids, and leave-behind materials
- Patient information leaflets
- Catalogs and price lists with product descriptions
- Websites associated with the product
- Training materials for healthcare providers
- Trade show displays
- Audiovisual materials presented in connection with the product

### 2.2 Definition of "Advertising"

The FD&C Act does not separately define "advertising" for devices (contrast § 502(n) for prescription drugs). For devices:

- **Advertising** generally refers to paid promotional communications in mass media (print, broadcast, digital) — for **restricted devices**, regulated directly by § 502(q) and (r)
- **Labeling** captures most other promotional materials under the misbranding provisions

> ⚠️ **Practical consequence:** Because most device promotional materials qualify as "labeling" under the broad *Kordel* interpretation, the misbranding provisions of § 502 govern the vast majority of device promotional activity — including materials that laypeople would call "advertising."

### 2.3 Labeling vs. Advertising — Regulatory Treatment

| Characteristic | Labeling | Advertising |
|----------------|----------|-------------|
| Statutory definition | FD&C Act § 201(m) | Not separately defined for devices |
| Primary regulation | FD&C Act § 502; 21 CFR Part 801 | FD&C Act § 502(q), (r); FTC Act §§ 5, 12 |
| Prescription/restricted device authority | FDA — primary | FDA — primary |
| OTC device authority | FDA — primary | FTC — primary (per 1971 MOU) |
| Pre-clearance required? | No | No (no device pre-clearance mechanism exists) |
| Review mechanism | Post-market surveillance; complaints; inspections | Post-market surveillance; complaints |

### 2.4 Contrast with Prescription Drug Advertising

This distinction is critical:

| Aspect | Prescription Drugs | Medical Devices |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Specific ad regulation | 21 CFR Part 202 | No equivalent |
| Brief summary requirement | Required for print ads (21 CFR 202.1(e)) | Not required (but § 502(r) requires a "brief statement" of intended uses + risks in restricted-device ads) |
| Pre-clearance of ads | Optional (OPDP advisory review) | No mechanism |
| At-dissemination submission | Form FDA 2253 | No device equivalent |
| Dedicated FDA office | OPDP (Office of Prescription Drug Promotion) | No equivalent office (CDRH compliance functions sit in OPEQ — see § 11.1) |
| Governing framework | 21 CFR Part 202 + FD&C Act | FD&C Act misbranding provisions (§ 502) |

#### 21 CFR 801.4 — Intended Uses (Deep Dive)

**21 CFR § 801.4** is the single most important provision for understanding how promotional activity can establish — or expand — a device's intended use. **Current text (as amended by the 2021 "Intended Uses" final rule, 86 FR 41383, effective September 1, 2021):**

> *"The words 'intended uses' or words of similar import ... refer to the objective intent of the persons legally responsible for the labeling of an article (or their representatives). The intent may be shown by such persons' expressions, the design or composition of the article, or by the circumstances surrounding the distribution of the article."*

And the critical proviso added in 2021:

> *"...a firm would not be regarded as intending an unapproved new use for an approved or cleared medical device **based solely on that firm's knowledge** that such device was being prescribed or used by health care providers for such use."*

Key principles established by § 801.4:
1. Intended use is **objective**, not solely the manufacturer's subjective intent
2. Promotional claims (expressions) — including oral statements by sales representatives — can **establish a new intended use**
3. **Mere knowledge** of off-label use, standing alone, does **not** establish intended use (2021 amendment) — but knowledge combined with other evidence (e.g., design changes, promotional conduct, distribution patterns) can
4. Once a new intended use is established, the device is legally subject to the requirements for that use (new 510(k)/De Novo/PMA)

🔗 [2021 Intended Uses Final Rule — 86 FR 41383](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2021/08/02/2021-16305/regulations-regarding-intended-uses)

#### 21 CFR 801.109 — Prescription Devices

Prescription devices must bear the legend: *"Caution: Federal law restricts this device to sale by or on the order of a ____"* — **or the alternative "Rx only" / "℞ only" symbol** (per § 801.109(b)(1), added by 81 FR 38911 (2016)), which is the predominant form in current use.

Sale is limited to licensed practitioners and facilities authorized to use/order the device. Advertising of restricted devices is separately governed by § 502(q)/(r) (see § 8).

## 4. The Intended Use Doctrine

### 4.1 Why Intended Use Matters

The concept of "intended use" is the **hinge point** of device advertising law. A device's regulatory status — including what indications it may be promoted for — depends entirely on its intended use. Promotional activity (including advertising, sales force communications, and even scientific literature distribution) can establish or expand intended use, triggering regulatory requirements.

### 4.2 How Intended Use Is Established

Per **21 CFR § 801.4** (as amended in 2021), intended use may be shown by:

| Source | Examples |
|--------|---------|
| **Express claims in labeling** | Statements in package inserts, IFUs, promotional brochures |
| **Advertising** | Claims in print ads, digital media, broadcast |
| **Oral statements** | Sales representative statements to HCPs |
| **Scientific/medical literature** | Reprints or publications provided by the manufacturer (but see SIUU safe-harbor policy, § 6.3) |
| **Design or composition** | Product features that fit only an unapproved use (added by 2021 rule) |
| **Circumstances of distribution** | Distribution channels, pricing, customer base |

**The 2021 knowledge proviso:** a firm is **not** regarded as intending an unapproved new use **based solely on knowledge** that HCPs are prescribing/using the device off-label. Knowledge plus additional conduct (promotion, design changes, targeting) remains fair game.

### 4.3 Promotional Activity as Evidence of Intended Use

This is the **core regulatory risk** in device promotion:

1. A 510(k)-cleared Class II diagnostic device is cleared for *in vitro* use
2. A sales representative routinely tells clinicians the device can be used intraoperatively
3. Those oral statements — even without a formal labeling change — are **expressions of objective intent** establishing a new intended use
4. The new intended use may require separate 510(k) clearance or PMA
5. Promotion for the new intended use = misbranding + possible adulteration (no clearance/approval for that use)

**Post-*Caronia* confirmation:** In ***United States v. Facteau***, 89 F.4th 1 (1st Cir. 2023), *cert. denied*, 145 S. Ct. 137 (2024), the First Circuit upheld misbranding convictions of Acclarent executives, holding that using **promotional speech as evidence of intended use** does not violate the First Amendment. This is the leading device-specific case reconciling *Caronia* with the intended-use doctrine.

### 4.4 Intended Use vs. Indications for Use

Though often used interchangeably, FDA distinguishes:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Intended use** | The general purpose or function of the device (21 CFR 801.4) |
| **Indications for use** | The specific disease/condition/patient population for which the device is cleared/approved |

The **Indications for Use** statement (**Form FDA 3881**) submitted with a 510(k) or PMA defines the specific claims a manufacturer may make in promotion. Going beyond this in advertising = misbranding risk.

🔗 [Form FDA 3881 — Indications for Use (PDF)](https://www.fda.gov/media/86323/download) · [510(k) Forms page](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/premarket-notification-510k/510k-forms)

### 4.5 Device vs. Drug Boundary

In ***Genus Medical Technologies v. FDA***, 994 F.3d 631 (D.C. Cir. 2021), the D.C. Circuit held that a product meeting the **device definition** must be regulated as a device, not a drug (except combination products) — a reminder that the promotional-claims framework that applies (drug ad rules vs. device misbranding rules) follows the product's regulatory classification.

## 5. Truthful and Non-Misleading Standard

### 5.1 The Core Standard

All medical device promotional materials must meet the standard established in **FD&C Act § 502(a)** and **§ 201(n)**:

- **Truthful** — factually accurate, based on adequate scientific substantiation
- **Non-misleading** — does not create a false impression, including by omission
- **Fairly balanced** — does not selectively present information

### 5.2 What Makes Claims Misleading? (§ 201(n))

FD&C Act § 201(n) specifically addresses misleading by omission:

> *"If an article is alleged to be misbranded because the labeling or advertising is misleading, then in determining whether the labeling or advertising is misleading there shall be taken into account (among other things) not only representations made or suggested by statement, word, design, device, or any combination thereof, but also the extent to which the labeling or advertising fails to reveal facts material in the light of such representations or material with respect to consequences which may result from the use of the article..."*

**Common sources of misleading claims:**

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Cherry-picking data** | Citing only favorable clinical endpoints; omitting primary endpoint failures |
| **Misleading statistics** | Reporting relative risk reduction without absolute risk reduction |
| **Context omission** | Claiming superiority without disclosing study population limitations |
| **Vague or absolute language** | "Eliminates infection risk"; "100% effective" |
| **Misleading visuals** | Before/after images not representative of typical outcomes |
| **Headline/body disconnect** | Headline implies broader benefit than body text supports |
| **Omission of contraindications** | Failing to disclose patient populations where device should not be used |
| **Implied claims** | Imagery or language that implies an uncleared use |
| **Population extrapolation** | Implying validated performance across populations not studied (see pulse oximeter case study, § 12.4) |

### 5.3 Fair Balance

Although "fair balance" as a formal regulatory requirement is specifically articulated for prescription drugs (21 CFR § 202.1), the **principle applies to device promotion** through the § 502(a) and § 201(n) standards, and FDA's **2009 draft guidance "Presenting Risk Information in Prescription Drug and Medical Device Promotion"** expressly covers device promotion.

Fair balance requires that:
- **Risks/warnings** are presented with reasonable prominence relative to benefit claims
- Contraindications, precautions, and limitations are not buried or minimized
- The overall impression is not misleadingly favorable

🔗 [Presenting Risk Information draft guidance (May 2009 — still draft as of 2026)](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/presenting-risk-information-prescription-drug-and-medical-device-promotion)

## 6. Off-Label Promotion and Scientific Communications

### 6.1 The General Prohibition

Manufacturers are generally **prohibited from promoting devices for uses not within the cleared or approved indications for use**. Such promotion:
- Establishes off-label intended use (21 CFR 801.4)
- Renders the device misbranded under FD&C Act § 502
- May render the device adulterated if it lacks premarket clearance/approval for the promoted use
- Can trigger criminal liability under § 303 (see *Facteau*, § 4.3)

### 6.2 Permissible Off-Label Communication Activities

Despite the general prohibition, certain activities are permissible under current law and FDA policy:

| Activity | Status | Governing Authority |
|----------|--------|--------------------|
| **Responding to unsolicited requests** from HCPs | Permissible within limits (private, non-public, tailored, scientific responses) | FDA draft guidance (Dec 2011 — still draft) |
| **Scientific information on unapproved uses (SIUU)** to HCPs | Permissible if guidance conditions met (source publications, disclosures, not false/misleading) | **FDA final guidance (Jan 6, 2025)** — see § 6.3 |
| **Communications consistent with FDA-required labeling (CFL)** | Permissible per guidance factors | FDA **final** guidance (June 2018) |
| **Health care economic information (HCEI)** to payors | Statutory safe harbor (§ 502(a)) — **extended to devices by CAA 2023 § 3630** | Statute + FDA payor guidance (final June 2018; revised draft June 2026) |
| **Pre-approval information exchange (PIE)** with payors | Statutory safe harbor — **§ 502(gg), added 2022** | CAA 2023 § 3630 |
| **Presentations at bona fide scientific conferences** | Generally permissible scientific exchange | FDA policy/guidance |
| **Medical/educational grants** | Permissible with safeguards against promotional intent | AdvaMed Code of Ethics; OIG fraud-and-abuse framework |

🔗 [Responding to Unsolicited Requests (draft, Dec 2011)](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/responding-unsolicited-requests-label-information-about-prescription-drugs-and-medical-devices)

### 6.3 The 2025 SIUU Final Guidance (Major Update)

***Communications From Firms to Health Care Providers Regarding Scientific Information on Unapproved Uses of Approved/Cleared Medical Products: Questions and Answers*** — **finalized January 6, 2025** (FR Doc. 2024-31539; docket FDA-2008-D-0053).

- Issued jointly by CDER, CBER, **CDRH**, CVM, and OC — **expressly covers approved/cleared devices** (510(k), De Novo, PMA)
- Supersedes the lineage of the 2009 "Good Reprint Practices" guidance (which followed the **sunset** of FDAMA § 401 in 2006)
- Covers firm-initiated sharing of **scientific reprints, clinical practice guidelines, reference texts, and firm-generated presentations** about unapproved uses, subject to conditions (truthful, non-misleading, scientifically sound, required disclosures, separation from promotional vehicles)
- Compliant SIUU communications will not, standing alone, be used as evidence of a new intended use
- **Caveat:** FDA notes the guidance is "not for current implementation" pending OMB review of information-collection provisions

🔗 [SIUU Final Guidance (Jan 2025)](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/communications-firms-health-care-providers-regarding-scientific-information-unapproved-uses)

### 6.4 HCEI and Payor Communications (Corrected Framework)

**Statute (§ 502(a), as amended):**
- **Cures § 3037 (2016)** established the HCEI safe harbor — but for **drugs only**
- **CAA 2023 (FDORA) § 3630** (Pub. L. 117-328, Dec 29, 2022) **extended HCEI to devices** and added **§ 502(gg)** (PIE)

**What HCEI actually requires** (§ 502(a)(1), (a)(2)):
- HCEI must **relate to an indication approved/cleared** for the product — § 502(a)(2)(B) provides HCEI "**does not include any analysis that relates only to an indication that is not approved**"
- Based on **competent and reliable scientific evidence**
- Must include, where applicable, **a conspicuous and prominent statement describing any material differences between the HCEI and the approved labeling**
- Recipients: payors, formulary committees, and similar entities

> ⚠️ **Correction vs. prior edition:** The prior edition said § 3037 lets manufacturers send HCEI about uses "not described in the approved labeling" with a disclosure that it involves unapproved uses. That is **wrong** — unapproved-use information to payors falls under the separate **§ 502(gg) PIE** safe harbor, not HCEI.

**§ 502(gg) — Pre-Approval Information Exchange (PIE):** permits truthful, non-misleading "product information" about an **investigational** product or use to be provided to payors, with enumerated disclosures (clear statement of unapproved status, development stage, non-selective presentation of study results, approved indications + labeling, updates if information becomes materially outdated).

**Guidance:** *Drug and Device Manufacturer Communications With Payors, Formulary Committees, and Similar Entities — Q&A*: **final June 12, 2018**; FDA issued a **revised draft on June 3, 2026** (91 FR 33181; comments due August 3, 2026) that will replace the 2018 final when finalized.

🔗 [Payor Communications Guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/drug-and-device-manufacturer-communications-payors-formulary-committees-and-similar-entities)

### 6.5 The First Amendment Dimension

- ***United States v. Caronia***, 703 F.3d 149 (2d Cir. 2012) — the government cannot criminalize **truthful, non-misleading** off-label promotion standing alone; FDA may still use speech as **evidence of intended use**
- ***Amarin Pharma, Inc. v. FDA***, 119 F. Supp. 3d 196 (S.D.N.Y. 2015) — preliminary injunction protecting truthful, non-misleading off-label communications
- ***Washington Legal Foundation v. Friedman***, 13 F. Supp. 2d 51 (D.D.C. 1998), vacated in relevant part, ***WLF v. Henney***, 202 F.3d 331 (D.C. Cir. 2000) — after FDA conceded its guidances were safe harbors with no independent prohibitory authority, the D.C. Circuit vacated without reaching the First Amendment merits
- ***United States v. Facteau***, 89 F.4th 1 (1st Cir. 2023), *cert. denied* (2024) — device-specific: promotional speech as **evidence of intended use** raises no First Amendment problem; misbranding convictions affirmed

> ⚠️ **Current Status:** Despite these rulings, FDA maintains that off-label promotion can still establish intended use and lead to regulatory action. FDA's 2025 SIUU guidance was accompanied by an updated First Amendment memorandum relying on *Facteau*. Companies should not rely on First Amendment arguments as a compliance strategy.

## 7. Evidence Standards for Promotional Claims

### 7.1 Substantiation Standards

Promotional claims must be adequately substantiated. The operative standard depends on the forum:

- **FDA (labeling + Rx/restricted device ads):** claims must be consistent with the data underlying clearance/approval and must not exceed the cleared/approved indication
- **FTC (OTC device ads):** "**competent and reliable scientific evidence**" — per the **FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance (Dec 2022)**, health benefit claims generally require **randomized controlled trials**, and substantiation must exist **before** the claim is disseminated

### 7.2 Comparative and Superiority Claims

Comparative claims ("superior to," "outperforms," "better than") face the highest evidentiary bar:

| Claim Type | Required Evidence |
|------------|------------------|
| **Superiority claim** | Head-to-head clinical trial with adequate statistical power; pre-specified primary endpoint |
| **Comparative effectiveness** | Equivalent methodology; contemporaneous comparison |
| **Cost-effectiveness** | Robust health economics model with validated inputs (and note HCEI/PIE rules for payor audiences) |
| **Non-inferiority** | Non-inferiority margin clinically justified and statistically met |

> ⚠️ Comparative claims based on indirect comparisons or meta-analyses typically do not meet FDA's evidentiary expectations for promotional purposes.

### 7.3 Performance Specifications in Promotion

When advertising performance specifications (e.g., sensitivity, specificity, accuracy, precision), manufacturers must:
1. Ensure specifications match the validated 510(k)/PMA performance data
2. Not extrapolate performance to populations not studied
3. Clearly identify the patient population and conditions under which performance was validated
4. Not imply performance in conditions different from validation conditions

## 8. Restricted Devices, Prescription Devices, and DTC Advertising

### 8.1 Restricted Device Framework

Under **FD&C Act § 520(e)** (21 U.S.C. § 360j(e)), FDA may restrict the sale, distribution, or use of a device if, without such restriction, it could present unreasonable risk. Restricted-device **advertising** is governed directly by **§ 502(q)** (false/misleading ads; violations of restriction orders) and **§ 502(r)** (required "brief statement" of intended uses and relevant warnings, precautions, side effects, and contraindications in all ads and descriptive printed matter).

### 8.2 Prescription Devices Under 21 CFR 801.109

Devices that are **not safe for use except under the supervision of a practitioner** licensed to use them are classified as prescription devices. Under **21 CFR § 801.109**:
- Required legend: *"Caution: Federal law restricts this device to sale by or on the order of a [licensed practitioner]"* **or the "Rx only"/"℞ only" symbol**
- Such devices may only be **sold to licensed practitioners**, hospitals, clinics, or other authorized entities

### 8.3 Direct-to-Consumer (DTC) Advertising for Devices (Corrected)

DTC advertising of restricted/prescription devices is **not per se prohibited**, but the framework is thin:

- FDA's **only** device-specific DTC guidance is the **draft "Consumer-Directed Broadcast Advertising of Restricted Devices" (Feb 10, 2004)** — **never finalized** and still FDA's sole statement on the topic
- DTC ads for restricted devices remain fully subject to **§ 502(q)** and **§ 502(r)** (including the brief-statement requirement)
- There is **no pre-clearance or at-dissemination submission** for device ads (contrast Form FDA 2253 for drugs)
- The **§ 303(g)** civil-penalty regime for DTC ads applies to **drugs/biologics only**, not devices
- FTC retains concurrent deceptive-advertising jurisdiction (and primary jurisdiction for OTC device ads)

> ⚠️ **Correction vs. prior edition:** The prior edition said DTC device ads "may require FDA review for certain device types" — no such review mechanism exists. Also note the internal tension the prior edition carried: Rx devices may be promoted to consumers (subject to § 502(q)/(r)) even though sale requires a prescription; FDA has publicly stated it regulates restricted-device advertising directed to both physicians and consumers.

**2025–2026 context:** In September 2025, HHS/FDA launched a major crackdown on deceptive **drug** DTC advertising (thousands of letters, ~100 cease-and-desists, and a proposed rule to eliminate the "adequate provision" mechanism for broadcast drug ads). Although drug-focused, it signals a heightened enforcement climate for DTC promotion generally. ([FDA press announcement, Sept 9, 2025](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/fda-launches-crackdown-deceptive-drug-advertising))

## 9. Social Media and Digital Promotion

### 9.1 General Principle

The fundamental rule: **all digital and social media promotional content is subject to the same regulatory standards as traditional promotional materials.** The format does not change the regulatory requirements.

🔗 [Character Space Limitations guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/internetsocial-media-platforms-character-space-limitations-presenting-risk-and-benefit-information)
🔗 [Addressing Misinformation Q&A (July 2024)](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/addressing-misinformation-about-medical-devices-and-prescription-drugs-questions-and-answers)

### 9.3 Key Rules for Specific Digital Channels

**Sponsored Search Links (Google, Bing)**
- Considered promotional labeling
- Must include product name AND risk information
- Cannot include unsubstantiated superiority claims
- Click-through landing page cannot present information inconsistent with labeling

**Social Media Posts (LinkedIn, Instagram, X/Twitter)**
- Character limitations do not excuse omission of risk/safety information
- Per the 2014 draft guidance, a character-limited post should present the most important risks alongside benefit claims, with a link to full risk information
- If a platform cannot accommodate benefit + risk + link, FDA's advice: reconsider using that platform

**Influencer and Physician KOL Posts**
- FTC requires **material connection disclosure** (16 CFR Part 255) — "sponsored," "#ad," "#partner"
- FDA's misbranding rules apply to manufacturer-sponsored content regardless of the speaker
- If a company compensates an HCP to post about a device, that post is company-sponsored promotion and must comply

**Company-Controlled Websites**
- Considered labeling — same standards as package inserts and brochures
- User-generated content (comments, reviews) on company-controlled platforms may create responsibility to correct misinformation (2024 misinformation draft guidance describes a voluntary approach)

**Webcasts and Virtual Promotional Programs**
- Same standard as in-person promotional programs
- Archived recordings remain subject to promotional rules indefinitely

### 9.4 AI-Generated Promotional Content

No FDA guidance specifically addresses AI-generated promotional content as of mid-2026:
- AI-generated claims are subject to the same truthfulness and non-misleading standards
- The manufacturer is responsible regardless of how content was generated
- AI "hallucinations" in a promotional context could constitute false/misleading statements
- **FTC context:** "Operation AI Comply" (Sept 2024) — FTC swept deceptive AI-claims marketers; per the FTC, "there is no AI exemption from the laws on the books" (see § 10.6)

### 9.5 Digital Health Boundary Guidance (Claims Implications)

Where a product's marketing claims push software across the device boundary, these guidances define the line:
- **General Wellness: Policy for Low Risk Devices** — **final, January 6, 2026** (supersedes 2019 version)
- **Clinical Decision Support Software** — **final, January 6, 2026** (supersedes Sept 2022 version) — implements Cures § 3060 (§ 520(o)) exclusions
- **Marketing Submission Recommendations for a Predetermined Change Control Plan for Artificial Intelligence-Enabled Device Software Functions** — final Dec 2024; current version Aug 2025
- **Artificial Intelligence-Enabled Device Software Functions: Lifecycle Management and Marketing Submission Recommendations** — draft, Jan 2025 (includes labeling/transparency recommendations relevant to AI claims)

🔗 [FDA Digital Health Center of Excellence](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/digital-health-center-excellence) · [Guidances with Digital Health Content](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/digital-health-center-excellence/guidances-digital-health-content)

## 10. FTC Jurisdiction and the FDA–FTC Interface

### 10.1 Division of Authority

| Device/Advertising Type | FDA Authority | FTC Authority |
|------------------------|---------------|---------------|
| Prescription/restricted device labeling | Primary | None |
| Prescription/restricted device advertising | Primary (§ 502(q)/(r)) | Concurrent (deceptive practices) |
| OTC/consumer device labeling | Primary | None |
| OTC/consumer device advertising | Concurrent (misbranding) | **Primary** |
| Cosmetic/device borderline products | Joint | Joint |

### 10.2 The 1971 FDA–FTC Memorandum of Understanding

MOU 225-71-8003 establishes jurisdictional coordination. Per the MOU: *"With exception of prescription drugs, the Federal Trade Commission has primary responsibility with respect to the regulation of the truth or falsity of all advertising (other than labeling) of foods, drugs, devices, and cosmetics."* FDA retains primary responsibility for labeling and for prescription-product advertising.

🔗 [FDA-FTC MOU 225-71-8003](https://www.fda.gov/about-fda/domestic-mous/mou-225-71-8003)

### 10.3 FTC Standards for OTC Device Advertising

The FTC's statutory foundation for device advertising:
- **FTC Act § 5** (15 U.S.C. § 45) — prohibits unfair or deceptive acts or practices
- **FTC Act § 12** (15 U.S.C. § 52) — makes it unlawful to disseminate false advertisements to induce purchase of **food, drugs, devices, services, or cosmetics** (the device-specific provision)

Core requirements:
1. **Truthful claims** — no false or misleading statements
2. **Substantiation before dissemination** — "competent and reliable scientific evidence"
3. **Clear and conspicuous disclosures** — material limitations, conditions, exceptions
4. **Endorsement compliance** — typical results (or disclosure); material connections disclosed
5. **No deception by omission**

🔗 [FTC Act — FTC.gov](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/statutes/federal-trade-commission-act)

### 10.4 FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance (December 2022) — Added

The **Health Products Compliance Guidance** (Dec 20, 2022) replaced the 1998 dietary-supplement advertising guide and covers **all health-related products — including medical devices, diagnostic tests, and health-related apps**. Key points:
- Health benefit claims require "competent and reliable scientific evidence" — generally **randomized controlled trials**
- Substantiation must exist **before** the claim is made
- Both express and implied claims require substantiation
- Consumer testimonials cannot substitute for scientific evidence

🔗 [FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/health-products-compliance-guidance)

### 10.5 FTC Endorsement Guides (16 CFR Part 255)

Updated **June 2023**, highly relevant to device marketing:
- Testimonials must reflect typical experiences or clearly disclose atypicality
- **Material connections** between endorsers and manufacturers must always be disclosed
- Health and safety claims via testimonials require the same substantiation as direct claims
- Applies to social media influencers, physician endorsers, patient advocates, celebrities — and advertisers/endorsers/intermediaries can all face liability

### 10.6 FTC Enforcement Examples (Added)

- **NeuroMetrix (Quell TENS device)** — March 2020, **$4 million** settlement; unsubstantiated body-wide pain-relief claims and "clinically proven"/FDA-clearance overreach
- **Willow Curve (low-level light therapy)** — June 2020, **$22 million** judgment (partially suspended); deceptive pain-relief and FDA-approval claims
- **Lumosity (brain training)** — January 2016, **$2 million** settlement; unsubstantiated cognitive claims
- **Operation AI Comply** — September 2024 sweep against deceptive AI claims: "there is no AI exemption from the laws on the books"

## 11. FDA Enforcement Mechanisms

### 11.1 Warning Letters

The most common formal enforcement tool for promotion violations.

**Issuing organization (updated):** Since CDRH's **October 2019 reorganization**, the Center no longer has an "Office of Compliance"; compliance functions sit in the **Office of Product Evaluation and Quality (OPEQ)**, with warning letters typically signed by OPEQ Office of Health Technology (OHT) directors, sometimes co-signed with ORA's Office of Medical Device and Radiological Health Operations (OMDRHO).

**Typical warning letter process:**
1. FDA inspection, complaint, or promotional review
2. FDA issues 483 observations (inspectional) or initiates a separate promotional review
3. Warning letter issued — responses **usually requested within 15 working days** (FDA Regulatory Procedures Manual, Ch. 4)
4. Company must describe corrective actions
5. FDA evaluates adequacy of response; may close out with a close-out letter

🔗 [FDA Warning Letter Database](https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/compliance-actions-and-activities/warning-letters)
🔗 [FDA Regulatory Procedures Manual](https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/regulatory-procedures-manual)

**Common grounds for device advertising warning letters:**
- Off-label promotion (promotion beyond cleared/approved indications)
- Unsubstantiated performance claims (e.g., unsupported sensitivity/specificity figures)
- False or misleading comparative claims
- Omission of required safety/risk information
- Promoting investigational devices outside the IDE framework
- Marketing an uncleared device (claims that create a device intended use with no 510(k)/De Novo/PMA)

### 11.2 Untitled Letters

Less severe than warning letters; used for violations that do not meet the warning-letter threshold. Requests voluntary corrective action.

### 11.3 Other Enforcement Tools (Corrected)

| Action | Authority | Description |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Injunction** | FD&C Act § 302 | Court order prohibiting continued violation |
| **Seizure** | FD&C Act § 304 | Removal of misbranded/adulterated goods from commerce |

## 12. Warning Letter and Enforcement Case Studies

### 12.1 Owlet Baby Care (2021) — Consumer Wearable Crossing into Device Claims

**Warning letter (CMS 616354, Oct 5, 2021):** FDA cited Owlet for marketing the **Smart Sock** — a consumer wearable measuring infants' blood oxygen (SpO2) and pulse rate with alarms — without marketing authorization. The product's claims (SpO2 monitoring with notifications) created a device intended use requiring 510(k)/De Novo authorization; FDA alleged adulteration and misbranding.

**Regulatory lesson:** Consumer "wellness" framing does not shield physiological-monitoring claims; the line is drawn by intended use established through marketing.

🔗 [Owlet Warning Letter](https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/warning-letters/owlet-baby-care-inc-616354-10052021)

### 12.2 Innova Medical Group (2021) — Unsupported Performance Claims

**Warning letter (CMS 614819, June 10, 2021):** FDA cited Innova for its unauthorized SARS-CoV-2 antigen test, including labeling claims of "96% sensitivity," "100% specificity," and "98.98% accuracy" that were unsupported — a textbook **§ 502(a)** false/misleading labeling case. A Class I recall followed.

**Regulatory lesson:** Performance numbers in labeling/promotion must match validated data; EUA-era shortcuts did not relax substantiation.

🔗 [Innova Warning Letter](https://www.fda.gov/inspections-compliance-enforcement-and-criminal-investigations/warning-letters/innova-medical-group-inc-614819-06102021)

### 12.3 Energy-Based "Vaginal Rejuvenation" Devices (2018) — Beyond Cleared Uses

On **July 30, 2018**, FDA announced a safety communication and letters to **seven manufacturers** (Alma Lasers, BTL Aesthetics, BTL Industries, Cynosure, InMode, Sciton, Thermigen) over **deceptive marketing of energy-based devices for unapproved "vaginal rejuvenation"** uses.

**Regulatory lesson:** Marketing a cleared device (e.g., for dermatology) for a new therapeutic use establishes a new intended use requiring new authorization; FDA will act on the promotional claims themselves.

🔗 [FDA Statement (July 30, 2018)](https://www.fda.gov/news-events/press-announcements/statement-fda-commissioner-scott-gottlieb-md-efforts-safeguard-womens-health-deceptive-health-claims)

### 12.4 Pulse Oximeter Accuracy (2021–2025) — Misleading by Omission Across Populations

Following evidence that pulse oximeters can overestimate oxygen saturation in patients with darker skin pigmentation:
- **Feb 19, 2021:** FDA Safety Communication on pulse oximeter accuracy and limitations
- **Jan 7, 2025:** FDA issued a **draft guidance** ("Pulse Oximeters for Medical Purposes — Non-Clinical and Clinical Performance Testing, Labeling, and Premarket Submission Recommendations") recommending performance evaluation across skin pigmentation and labeling disclosures

**Regulatory lesson:** Stating accuracy specifications validated primarily in light-skinned populations — without disclosing the limitation — is **misleading by omission under § 201(n)**. (Note: no pulse-oximeter warning letters have been identified; the prior edition's claim of such letters was removed as unverifiable.)

🔗 [FDA Safety Communication (Feb 2021)](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/safety-communications/pulse-oximeter-accuracy-and-limitations-fda-safety-communication)

### 12.5 COVID-19 Fraudulent Products (2020–2023) — Corrected Picture

FDA's fraudulent-COVID-19 page lists **~220 letters (March 2020–January 2023)** — the **majority targeted unapproved drugs/supplements** claiming to prevent or treat COVID-19. Device-relevant actions included:
- Unauthorized diagnostic tests (Innova 6/10/21; Lepu 7/29/21; Empowered Diagnostics 10/6/22)
- **13 face-mask warning letters (2021)** over misleading impressions of FDA approval
- Thermal-imaging camera letters (3/4/21)

**Regulatory lesson:** Emergency Use Authorization ≠ approval; claiming "FDA approved" for an EUA product is false/misleading; EUA conditions of use bound all promotion.

🔗 [FDA Fraudulent COVID-19 Products](https://www.fda.gov/consumers/health-fraud-scams/fraudulent-coronavirus-disease-2019-covid-19-products)

- **FTC v. Lumos Labs (Lumosity), Jan 2016** — $2M settlement over unsubstantiated brain-training claims (FTC, not FDA, action)
- **FDA CES reclassification (Dec 20, 2019)** — cranial electrotherapy stimulators: anxiety/insomnia reclassified to Class II with special controls; **depression claims require PMA** — a direct example of claims driving classification

## 13. Device Class- and Pathway-Specific Rules

### 13.1 Class I Devices (General Controls)

- Must comply with 21 CFR Part 801 general labeling requirements
- Most are 510(k)-exempt — but exemption is **bound by the limitations of exemption** (21 CFR 862–892, ".9" provisions): claims exceeding the exemption category require a 510(k)
- No premarket review of advertising claims
- Examples: bandages, tongue depressors, hand-held surgical instruments

**Key risk:** making performance claims that elevate a Class I device's apparent risk profile beyond general controls — requiring clearance not obtained.

### 13.2 Class II Devices (Special Controls)

- 21 CFR Part 801 + applicable special controls
- 510(k) clearance defines the **permissible scope of promotional claims**
- The **Indications for Use** in the 510(k) defines maximum permissible claims
- Examples: diagnostic imaging devices, infusion pumps, powered wheelchairs

**Promotional limit:** substantial equivalence to a predicate does not let a manufacturer promote for the predicate's indications unless those indications are also cleared in the device's own 510(k).

### 13.3 Class III Devices (PMA)

- Most stringent requirements
- All proposed labeling reviewed as part of PMA (21 CFR Part 814)
- Post-approval **labeling supplements** required for changes to approved labeling
- Promotional claims must conform to PMA-approved labeling

🔗 [FDA PMA Database](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfpma/pma.cfm)

### 13.4 De Novo Devices (History Corrected)

- Created by **FDAMA 1997 § 207** (FD&C Act § 513(f)(2)) — reclassification of novel low-to-moderate-risk devices from automatic Class III
- **FDASIA 2012 § 607** created the **direct De Novo** pathway (no prior 510(k)/NSE needed)
- The De Novo authorization letter establishes classification, indications, and special controls; promotional claims are bounded by the authorization

> ⚠️ **Correction vs. prior edition:** De Novo is not a "21st Century Cures Act modernization" — it dates to 1997, with the direct pathway added in 2012.

🔗 [FDA De Novo Database](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfpmn/denovo.cfm)

### 13.5 Humanitarian Device Exemption (HDE) — Corrected

Under **FD&C Act § 520(m)** and **21 CFR Part 814, Subpart H**:
- HDE devices address diseases/conditions affecting **not more than 8,000** individuals per year in the U.S. (raised from 4,000 by Cures § 3052)
- **Profit cap (§ 520(m)(3)):** the device may not be sold for more than the costs of R&D, fabrication, and distribution — **except** the § 520(m)(6) pediatric-population exception (currently authorized for requests submitted on or before **October 1, 2027**)
- **IRB oversight:** use is subject to institutional review board approval/continuing review (§ 520(m)(4); 21 CFR 814.124)
- **Required labeling (21 CFR 814.104(b)(4)(ii)):** *"Humanitarian Device. Authorized by Federal law for use in the [treatment/diagnosis] of [disease/condition]. The effectiveness of this device for this use has not been demonstrated."*

> ⚠️ **Correction vs. prior edition:** There is **no statutory or regulatory "prohibition on advertising"** for HDE devices. The real constraints are the profit cap, IRB oversight, and mandatory labeling statements. Promotional claims obviously cannot contradict the required "effectiveness has not been demonstrated" statement without being misleading under § 502(a).

### 13.6 Investigational Devices (IDE)

Under **21 CFR § 812.7**, investigational devices may **not be promoted or commercialized**:
- May not be commercially distributed or sold
- May not be represented as safe or effective for the uses under investigation
- Recruitment communications limited to information needed for enrollment
- **Payor communications about investigational products:** the **§ 502(gg) PIE** safe harbor now provides a statutory channel (see § 6.4)

### 13.7 OTC Hearing Aids (Added)

- **FDARA 2017 § 709** added FD&C Act § 520(q), creating an OTC hearing aid category for adults with perceived mild-to-moderate hearing loss
- **2022 final rule (87 FR 50698):** 21 CFR § 800.30 (OTC controls/labeling/conditions for sale) and § 801.422 (prescription hearing aid labeling); former §§ 801.420–.421 removed
- Marketing lesson: claims that an unregistered "personal sound amplification product" (PSAP) treats hearing loss cross the device line

## 14. Combination Products

### 14.1 Jurisdictional Assignment

Combination products are governed by **21 CFR Part 3**. The primary mode of action (PMOA) determines the lead center (§ 3.2(m)):

| PMOA | Lead Center | Advertising Rules |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Drug-primary | CDER | 21 CFR Part 202 (drug ad rules) |
| Device-primary | CDRH | FD&C Act § 502 misbranding rules (incl. § 502(q)/(r) if restricted) |
| Biologic-primary | CBER | Biologic promotion rules |

**21 CFR Part 4** governs **cGMPs (Subpart A) and postmarketing safety reporting (Subpart B)** for combination products — it does **not** select advertising rules; promotion-rule choice follows the Part 3 lead-center assignment.

🔗 [21 CFR Part 3 — eCFR](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-21/chapter-I/subchapter-A/part-3)
🔗 [FDA Office of Combination Products](https://www.fda.gov/combination-products)

### 14.2 Common Combination Product Advertising Challenges

- **Drug-device combinations** (prefilled syringes, drug-eluting stents, inhalers): promotion governed by drug rules if drug-primary; device misbranding rules if device-primary
- **Software as a Medical Device (SaMD):** promotional claims about AI/ML capabilities are subject to device advertising standards; note the Cures § 520(o) software-function exclusions and the CDS guidance boundary (§ 9.5)
- **Device vs. drug boundary:** *Genus Medical Technologies v. FDA* (D.C. Cir. 2021) — products meeting the device definition are regulated as devices

## 17. Private Litigation and Other Exposure (New Section)

### 17.1 Lanham Act § 43(a) — Competitor False Advertising

The **Lanham Act, 15 U.S.C. § 1125(a)**, gives competitors a private right of action for false or misleading representations in commercial advertising. In ***POM Wonderful LLC v. Coca-Cola Co.***, 573 U.S. 102 (2014), the Supreme Court held that FDCA regulation does **not** preclude Lanham Act claims (a food-labeling case, applied across FDA-regulated industries). Device companies can sue — and be sued by — competitors over promotional claims even where FDA has not acted.

### 17.2 State Consumer Protection Laws

State UDAP statutes (e.g., California's UCL/FAL) allow consumers, competitors, and state AGs to challenge deceptive device advertising, often with class-action exposure. State claims parallel federal substantiation standards and can survive alongside FDA compliance.

### 17.3 Other Exposure

- **FTC/DOJ civil penalties** for FTC Act violations (15 U.S.C. § 45(m))
- **SEC/disclosure risk** for public companies making promotional claims inconsistent with regulatory status
- **Whistleblower (qui tam) suits** under the FCA for off-label promotion driving federal-program claims

## 19. Compliance Best Practices

### 19.1 Medical-Legal-Regulatory (MLR) Review Process

Every promotional material should pass through a formal **MLR review** before use:

| Role | Review Focus |
|------|-------------|
| **Medical/Clinical** | Clinical accuracy; fair balance; study data interpretation |
| **Legal** | False advertising risk (incl. Lanham Act); competitor claims; IP; litigation risk |
| **Regulatory** | Alignment with cleared/approved labeling; CFR compliance; intended use scope |

**Key MLR process elements:**
1. Documented SOP for promotional material review
2. Clear routing/approval workflow with audit trail
3. Version control and archiving of all materials with approval records
4. Date-stamped records of when materials were in use
5. Periodic review and retirement of outdated materials (note QMSR/ISO 13485 document-control alignment since Feb 2026)

### 19.2 Sales Force Training and Monitoring

| Area | Best Practice |
|------|--------------|
| **Initial training** | Approved indications, off-label rules (incl. 2021 knowledge proviso nuances), product-specific claims |
| **Promotional materials** | Provide only approved, MLR-cleared materials |
| **Off-label requests** | Written SOP for unsolicited-request responses; route to medical affairs |
| **Monitoring** | Regular ride-alongs; call audits; sales-meeting monitoring |
| **Reporting** | Clear escalation process for off-label inquiries or complaints |
| **Speaker programs** | MLR-cleared decks; speaker training; fair-market-value compensation (AKS) |

### 19.3 Claim Substantiation Files

For every promotional claim, maintain a substantiation file containing:
- Scientific/clinical evidence supporting the claim (RCT-level where comparative/health-benefit claims — FTC standard)
- Statistical analysis supporting quantitative claims
- Reference to cleared/approved labeling supporting the claim
- MLR approval documentation
- Date range the material was in use

### 19.4 Digital Asset Management

- Digital library of all promotional materials with version history
- Expiration/review dates on all digital assets
- Websites subject to the same MLR review as print
- Monitor social channels for employee/third-party posts attributable to the company
- Policies for AI-assisted content generation (human verification before release)

## 22. Self-Assessment Questions

**Statutory/Regulatory Framework**
1. What FD&C Act provision is the primary basis for FDA enforcement against false or misleading device advertising? How does this differ from the drug advertising regulatory framework?
2. A manufacturer of a Class II diagnostic imaging device wants to expand its use to a new anatomical site. What regulatory considerations govern whether this expansion can be promoted?
3. Under 21 CFR 801.4 (as amended in 2021), what evidence can FDA use to establish a device manufacturer's intended use? What is the significance of the "knowledge" proviso?

**Intended Use / Off-Label Promotion**
4. A sales representative consistently tells orthopedic surgeons that a cleared bone screw can be used for a procedure not in the 510(k) indications. What are the regulatory implications? What are the legal risks?
5. A medical device manufacturer receives an unsolicited call from a physician asking about using the device in a pediatric population not studied. How should the manufacturer respond under FDA's 2011 draft guidance?
6. How did *United States v. Caronia* and *United States v. Facteau* shape the legal landscape for off-label promotion? What is FDA's current policy position?
7. What is the difference between HCEI under § 502(a) and PIE under § 502(gg)? Which statute extended each to devices?

**Evidence Standards**
8. A Class III cardiac device manufacturer wants to run an ad claiming its device is "superior to" a competitor's device. What evidence standard applies? What types of data would be required?
9. Describe the difference between FDA's substantiation expectations and the FTC's "competent and reliable scientific evidence" standard under the 2022 Health Products Compliance Guidance. When does each apply?

**Digital/Social Media**
10. A company posts a 280-character post about its cleared diagnostic device that includes only efficacy claims with a link to the full labeling. Does this comply with FDA's social media draft guidance? What is missing?
11. A physician who receives a consulting fee from a device manufacturer posts on Instagram about the device without disclosing the relationship. What regulations are potentially violated and by whom?

**Enforcement**
12. What is the difference between a Warning Letter and an Untitled Letter in the CDRH enforcement context? What actions should a company take upon receiving each?
13. How can off-label promotion of a medical device lead to False Claims Act liability? Cite one real device example.

**International**
14. Under EU MDR Article 7, what is the standard for permissible device claims? How does this compare to the U.S. truthful and non-misleading standard under FD&C Act § 502(a)?

### Answer Outlines

1. § 502(a) (false/misleading labeling) plus § 502(q)/(r) for restricted-device ads; devices lack a Part 202-equivalent regulation, a brief-summary rule, an OPDP-equivalent office, and any pre-clearance mechanism.
2. New anatomical site = new intended use/indication (21 CFR 801.4); assess whether a new 510(k) is required (see 2017 "Deciding When to Submit a 510(k)" guidance); promoting before clearance = misbranding/adulteration.
3. Expressions (labeling, ads, oral statements), design/composition, circumstances of distribution. The 2021 proviso: knowledge alone of off-label use by HCPs does not establish intended use.
4. Rep's statements are expressions of objective intent establishing a new intended use; device becomes misbranded (§ 502) and potentially adulterated (no clearance for that use); risk of warning letter, injunction, § 303 criminal exposure, FCA liability; cf. *Facteau*.
5. Under the 2011 draft guidance: treat as unsolicited request — provide a private, non-public, truthful, balanced, scientific response tailored to the question; document it; do not use the request as a springboard for promotion.
6. *Caronia*: truthful, non-misleading off-label speech can't be criminalized standing alone. *Facteau* (device case): using that speech as evidence of intended use is constitutional. FDA's position: enforce via intended-use/misbranding theory; SIUU guidance provides a compliant channel.
7. HCEI (§ 502(a)): economic info relating to an approved/cleared indication, for payors; extended to devices by CAA 2023 § 3630. PIE (§ 502(gg), same statute): truthful info about investigational products/uses to payors with required disclosures.
8. Head-to-head, adequately powered, well-controlled clinical trial with pre-specified endpoints; claims must also stay within approved labeling; comparative claims are a frequent warning-letter trigger.
9. FTC: competent and reliable scientific evidence, generally RCTs for health benefit claims, substantiation required before dissemination — applies to OTC device advertising (primary) and concurrently elsewhere; FDA: consistency with data underlying clearance/approval and cleared/approved labeling — applies to labeling and restricted/Rx device promotion.
10. No. Per the 2014 character-space draft guidance: the most important risk information must appear in the post itself alongside benefits, with a link to complete risk information; benefit-only + link is insufficient. If the platform can't accommodate both, reconsider the platform.
11. FTC Act § 5 and 16 CFR Part 255 (undisclosed material connection) — exposure for both the physician-endorser and the manufacturer; the post is also manufacturer-sponsored promotion subject to FDA misbranding rules; potential AKS issues in the underlying arrangement.
12. Warning letter: formal notice of significant violations, usually 15 working days to respond, posted publicly; demands corrective action plan. Untitled letter: lower-threshold violations, requests voluntary correction. Both warrant prompt, documented responses and MLR/root-cause review.
13. Off-label promotion that causes providers to submit federal-program claims for non-covered uses can render those claims "false" under the FCA (31 U.S.C. § 3729); examples: Medtronic/Kyphon ($75M, 2008); ev3/Onyx ($50.9M incl. criminal plea, 2018); Synthes/Norian criminal pleas (2010).
14. Article 7 prohibits claims (expressly including advertising) that mislead as to intended purpose, safety, or performance — ascribing nonexistent properties, creating false treatment/diagnostic impressions, failing to inform of likely risks, or suggesting uses beyond the conformity-assessed purpose. Conceptually parallel to § 502(a)/§ 201(n) (truthful, non-misleading, no material omissions), but MDR ties claims to the manufacturer's declared "intended purpose" (Art. 2(12)), which itself is defined partly by promotional materials.
